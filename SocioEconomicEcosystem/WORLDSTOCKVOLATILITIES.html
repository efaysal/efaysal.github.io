Learning Depth Volatility Patterns

In Major World Stock Market Indices

Using Algorithmic Data Driven Analytics

Faysal.El.Khettabi@gmail.com

    Introduction:

    The stock market volatility clustering, excess and “long memory” behavior are recognized as stylized properties present in most financial time series from stock market data.

    Various modeling methods have been proposed to highlight and provide insights about these volatility characteristics in terms of economical conditions, behavior of market participants and the news event process, these methods were focused on estimating the stock volatility not necessary well-calibrated, the estimated volatilities of a number of different stocks may not be comparable.

    Research Points:

    An essential element in the computational process is to provide comparable numerical quantities. Given a number of different stock markets, the quantified volatility characteristics as tendencies & trends should be calculated using a modeling process of reducing measurements to a "neutral" or "standard" scale, i.e important parameters in the learning process should have the same scale for a "fair" comparison between these different stock markets.

    Its implementation can be built up gradually over time and in response to specific social & economical conditions(events), the parameters identification and its quantification are feasible or releasable, i.e. the magnitude of the change in the volatility should be binding to these events and is required to be an accurate quantification of their impact ( less biased).

    Developmental Data Driven Analytics:

    Our Algorithmic Data Driven Analytics(ADDA) takes a descriptive approach to studying properties of a stock market that follow from empirical observations(data) to score the stock volatility tendencies & trends under a "neutral" or "standard" scale. The numerical results related to major World stock market indices, since 1995 up to recently, are presented in this Interactive Chart, (a double click on a stock shows its depth volatility score curve).

    ﻿Upward spikes in volatility are mainly associated to sharp stock market sell-offs. Declines in volatility are associated to steady gains in the market and tend to unfold slowly. This cyclic volatility starts to be extraordinarily visible after the Financial crisis of 2007–2008. Whether across time, or across World stock markets, there is a strikingly high correlation between the depth volatility patterns over a period of 10 years.
