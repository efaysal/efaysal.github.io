    IIROC Marketplace Thresholds:

    The Investment Industry Regulatory Organization of Canada (IIROC) published guidance on the establishment and operation of price thresholds by Canadian marketplaces to further reduce short-term, unexplained price volatility and address risks arising from electronic trading. The guidance took effect on August 25, 2016.

    Comment:

    Mainly, the process proposed a price threshold schema as a rational expectation to reduce the volatility and stabilize the stock. This process may be severely challenged to ensure consistent volatility control mechanism in pricing models involving uncertainty. It is unclear how this proposed process is controlling the volatility without relying on stochastic process driving stock prices.

    Our starting point is the fact that the tendencies and trends in volatility are best gauged not only by the proposed price threshold process but also by the randomness-type properties emerging in the considered financial market where the price formation process is taking form. For compelling assessment, we should be concerned with these randomness-type properties and not to be ignored. These randomness-type properties combined with temporal price changes do also contribute to risk emergence by forming a "systemic" volatility hindering more the stock stability.

    One can try to achieve clarity by considering known stochastic assumptions in the conventional stochastic financial framework to reformulate the problem as a quasi-deterministic framework highlighting and providing insights about the volatility characteristics in terms of stock settings, the used price threshold schema or any used process for "Volatility Control Mechanism" in the considered financial market.
    A more effective application of this quasi-deterministic framework is to assess empirically these Volatility Control Mechanisms.

    Data Driven Analytics To Estimate Depth Volatility Patterns:

    For this end, we developed descriptive approaches to studying properties of a stock market volatility that follow from empirical observations(realized quotes), i.e., to infer historical volatility structures from data and to score the stock volatility tendencies & trends under a "neutral" or "standard" scale. The historical volatility is decomposed into two "degrees of variation" caused by oscillatory processes which occur accordingly to different origins and mechanisms.

    This certainly is a sufficient motivation for the fact that, in this work, we consider these type of oscillatory processes for modeling the observed/historical volatility and to assess if these oscillatory processes are the results of the stock settings and the used process for "Volatility Control Mechanism" in the considered financial market.

    Some Observations:

    ﻿﻿Upward spikes in volatility tendencies & trends are mainly associated to sharp stock market sell-offs. Declines in volatility are associated to steady gains in the market and tend to unfold with irregular bursts with a more or less random sequence of patterns, this was before the year 2007. After that year, our numerical result is showing cyclical volatility tendencies & trends in various ways, see Oscillatory Process I.
    This cyclic volatility starts to be extraordinarily visible after the Financial crisis of 2007–2008. For instance, major Canadian bank stocks(BMO, BNS, CM, TD, RY) started showing more correlated Oscillatory Processes I&II after the crisis. This is a puzzling satiation?
    Numerous stocks, including different economic sectors, exhibit widely range of values in the estimated volatilities.
