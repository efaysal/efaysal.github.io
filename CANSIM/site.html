    Debt Analytics & Metrics:

    Thanks to low interest rate monetary policy, most publicly economical or financial data services provided by the developed countries show that the households(also other economical entities) are highly leveraged. The most salient feature of this landscape is a challenge to the central bank's monetary policies, how to handle future interest rate level, how to control the impact of their previous and coming decisions on economic activities, financial stability and especially on households equities, i.e. future wealth and income redistributions. Any resulting uncertainties can not necessary influence household decisions in an rational way, households may start reducing their current consumption for more savings, if any, against possible fluctuations in debt service ratios.

    The question we need to examine:

    Are these current leverage ratios optimal?

    • In the sense that it minimizes the cost of capital for households economical activities, investing in real estate for instance.
    • Without acquiring a high possibility of default during an economic downturn or especially if interest rates start to rise again as more debt means higher debt service ratios.
    In this work, the optimal leverage ratio dynamics are compared to the current leverage ratio dynamics and a statistical assessment is conducted to see if the new indicator related to the debt service ratio is appropriately reflecting an early warning indicator. The methodology, the data inputs and some results are discussed (high-level overview) in the remainder of this essay.

    Nomenclature & Definition:

    In this essay, the leverage ratio is defined as the "amount" of liability devided by the "amount" of the equity. In financial accounting, see International Financial Reporting Standards - IFRS , a balance sheet covers the assets as liabilities plus the ownership equity. The System of National Accounts SNA provides formal definition in Chapter 3: Stocks, flows and accounting rules.

    A balance sheet may be understood as a "snapshot" of the financial condition of an economic entity at single point in time, yearly or quarterly as the case of Cansim framework which is following mainly IFRS and The System of National Accounts SNA standards for its data preparation.

    The debt service ratio-DSR is defined as the ratio of interest payments plus amortisations to income, more details are in this publication of the Bank for International Settlements BIS with a mandate to promote global monetary and financial stability through international cooperation.

    Methodology:

    To derive an optimal leverage ratio, we used a variant formulation of the interesting framework in "Stochastic Optimal Control and Financial Modeling" . Our approach derived interpretable and compatible formulations as in some works under this second rich framework: "Probability and Finance".

    As the original formulation requires extra or intermediate level-datasets which are not always fully available, hindered in their estimations by a lack of adequate data resources or need to satisfy several additional requirements.

    Our reformulation enbales us to compute the optimal leverage ratio as an inverse problem, i.e. calculating from a set of observations the causal factors that produced them and its numerical implementation is encouraging and simplifies the numerical task considerably as it lessens the need for extra or intermediate level-datasets fully required in the original formulation.

    Note that our approaches assess the degree of uncertainty by looking at the volatility of economic or financial indicators related to the temporal dynamics of the balance sheet. For example, the variation of debt indices, the evolution of recent changes in debt to gross domestic product, debt to disposable income, debt to net worth, debt to financial assets, debt to NON-financial assets and so on.

    Data & Numerics:


    Our numerical results rely as much as possible on the compiled data by the Canadian System of National Accounts and are available through public repositories, Cansim server.

    Our approximated calculation will mainly affect the level of the feature under consideration rather than its dynamics, by using the same analytical form and input data series over time, it generates a feature having a time series that captures the important dynamical pattern of the feature under consideration. This approximation is more useful when a calibration or normalization are used to compare the numerical level values of two similar features. In this work, we use zscore normalization: zscore = (Value â€“Mean)/sd, where "Value" is the raw numerical value, "Mean" is the average value of the time series and "sd" is the standard deviation, zscore describes where a value is located in the distribution. For instance, a zscore of 0 is at the mean of the distribution and a zscore of 2.0 or beyond is in the tails of the distribution. A negative zscore means that the original score is below the mean and positive is under the mean. For more detail, For more details, see Standard score.
